The Basics of Why Stocks Make You Rich

The Secret to Long Lasting Wealth

Investers such as the likes of Warren Buffet have been involved with the stock market for decades. The secret to their immense wealth has to do with a concept called compound interest.

Let's break this concept down for a moment. Let's say you are interested in buying a stock of "Company Y" for $10.00 a share today and you buy 10 shares of said stock. You now own $100.00 worth of "Company Y" shares. The average annualized return adjusted for inflation of the stock market over a 30 year span is roughly 8.3%. Based on this average, after your first year holding these shares, they will now be worth $108.30 assuming you did not "dollar average" and buy more shares over the course of this first year. Now, instead of earning 8.3% interest on your initial $100.00, you will gain 8.3% of $108.30, resulting in your shares of "Company Y" being worth $117.29.

Why is it important to gain interest on your capital? The simple answer is inflation. The average inflation rate of the U.S. dollar per year over the last 30 years has been roughly 2% (conservatively). What this means is for every dollar you have sitting in cash or in a checking account becomes worth $0.98 worth of spending power the following year. So if you are not gaining atleast 2% interest on your savings every year, you are actually getting poorer every year even if you do not spend a penny.
